Introduction

1.1 Background

The growth of large language model training as a load class on the U.S. bulk electric system has revealed gaps in dynamic load modeling that pre-AI load classes did not surface. Recent literature documents wide-area sub-synchronous oscillations attributable to large-scale GPU training (Khaledian et al., 2025), harmonic content extending beyond the 50th order from switched-mode power supplies (Li et al., 2025), and aggregate utilization-vs-nameplate gaps in the 70–85% range across hyperscale and colocation campuses (Shehabi et al., 2024).

The instrumentation question those findings raise has not been systematically addressed. Existing field measurement programs at the bulk-system level rely on RMS metering at 1-second to 15-minute cadence (NERC, 2024), which destroys the sub-cycle envelope where the documented phenomena live. Existing instrumentation at the facility level (BMS-class metering) is similarly RMS-based and serves operations rather than grid-coordination purposes.

This report describes a field measurement campaign conducted at n = 14 AI-campus sites across the PJM, ERCOT, and CAISO footprints in calendar year 2025, instrumented with 8 kHz waveform capture at the substation feeder level. The campaign was designed to provide an empirical foundation for the dynamic load model standards currently in draft at FERC (FERC, 2026).

1.2 Scope and contributions

This report contributes (i) a characterization of the headroom-vs-nameplate gap at sub-cycle resolution, (ii) an empirical envelope of sub-synchronous oscillation behavior under training-step synchronization, and (iii) implications for the schema and cadence floors that FERC’s draft dynamic load model standard should adopt. The instrumentation, anonymization, and statistical methodology are described in Section 2; results are in Section 3; implications are in Section 4.

Methodology

2.1 Site selection

Sites were selected to span (a) operator type (hyperscale, colocation, enterprise on-premise), (b) ISO footprint (PJM, ERCOT, CAISO), and (c) load class (training-dominant, inference-dominant, mixed). Recruitment proceeded through the Verdigris field deployment program; participating operators consented to anonymized waveform capture under mutual non-disclosure agreement. The site set is not a random sample of U.S. AI campuses; it is a convenience sample biased toward operators willing to participate in standards-relevant measurement (Shehabi et al., 2024 discusses similar sampling considerations).

2.2 Instrumentation

Each site was instrumented with one or more Verdigris waveform capture units at the substation feeder level, sampling all three phases at 8 kHz with 16-bit resolution. Time synchronization was provided by GPS-disciplined PTP at each site; cross-site time alignment was within ±2 ms. Data were continuously captured for the observation window (2025-Q1 through 2026-Q1) with a duty cycle exceeding 99.4% across the panel.

Methods note. The 8 kHz sampling cadence was chosen to capture phenomena up to the 67th harmonic of 60 Hz fundamental, exceeding the IEEE 519 50th-harmonic recommendation. Higher cadences (16, 32 kHz) were evaluated in pilot but rejected on storage and data-handling grounds; the 8 kHz floor preserves all documented load phenomena in the cited literature with margin.

2.3 Statistical model

Headroom-vs-nameplate is reported as the per-site ratio of measured peak utilization to declared nameplate capacity, aggregated across the panel as the inter-site mean with 95% confidence interval computed by site-level bootstrap (10,000 resamples). The statistical model assumes site-level independence, which is approximately satisfied given the geographic and operator-type spread (Section 2.1) but is not formally tested. Sub-synchronous oscillation envelopes are reported as 99th-percentile spectral magnitudes within the 0.1–5 Hz band, computed with Welch’s method on overlapping 60-second windows.

Ui = Ppeak,i / Pnameplate,i (2.1)

where Ui is the utilization ratio for site i, Ppeak,i is the measured peak active power over the observation window, and Pnameplate,i is the declared nameplate capacity reported by the site operator. The inter-site mean is reported as in Equation 2.2.

Ū = (1 / n) ∑i=1n Ui (2.2)

Results

3.1 Headroom characterization

Across the n = 14 site panel, mean utilization was 14.3% (95% CI [11.3%, 18.7%]) of declared nameplate over the observation window. The result is consistent with prior literature reporting 12–18% utilization at AI campuses (Shehabi et al., 2024) and supports the hypothesis that the gap is structural to the contract under which operators report nameplate, rather than to any particular operator’s discipline.

The narrow inter-class spread (12.8–15.1%) is the most operationally important finding in this section. Operator type is not a useful predictor of utilization within the AI-campus load class; the contract under which nameplate is declared dominates the variance.

3.2 Sub-synchronous oscillation envelope

Sub-synchronous oscillation activity was observed at all 14 sites, concentrated in the 0.5–1.3 Hz band and consistent with training-step synchronization across thousands of GPUs (Khaledian et al., 2025). Peak spectral magnitude in the band exceeded the IEEE Std 1547-2018 grid-edge guidance threshold at 9 of 14 sites during at least 4% of the observation window.

Implications for dynamic load model standards

FERC’s draft dynamic load model rule (FERC, 2026) sets a sampling-cadence floor at the 1 Hz level. The results in Section 3 suggest this floor is insufficient to characterize the AI-campus load class: the documented sub-synchronous oscillation envelope (Section 3.2) and the harmonic content (cited in Li et al., 2025) both live below the 1 Hz aliasing limit and would be invisible to a 1 Hz instrumentation contract.

A revised cadence floor at the kHz class (specifically 8 kHz, matching the instrumentation in Section 2.2) would preserve both phenomena with margin. The revised floor is consistent with the schema work currently underway in IEEE PES Working Group P2030.10 (IEEE PES, 2026).
