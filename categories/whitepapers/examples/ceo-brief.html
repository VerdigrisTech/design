CEO BRIEFRESEARCH & POLICY AUDIENCE

Telemetry as Contract,
Not Feature

Behind-the-meter visibility is the load-bearing primitive for an AI-factory grid. Treating it as a procurement line item is how the next decade gets stranded.

Mark Chung CEO, Verdigris Technologies, Los Altos CA
May 2026
Disclosure. The author is CEO of Verdigris Technologies, which develops the telemetry products discussed. Views are the author’s.
01

The argument

For thirty years, behind-the-meter telemetry has been sold to facility operators the way analytics dashboards are sold to product teams: a feature on a contract, a line item next to switchgear and BMS hardware. That framing made sense when the load was a cold-storage warehouse and the question was whether the chiller cycle had drifted. It does not survive contact with an AI training cluster whose load profile changes character three times before the meter completes a 15-minute integration window.

The thesis of this brief is narrow and load-bearing. Behind-the-meter telemetry is no longer a feature. It is a contract between the facility and the grid. The contract specifies a sampling cadence, a schema, an availability window, and a recipient. Without that contract, every layer above it (forecasts, dynamic load models, dispatch agents, capacity-market clearing) operates on a stale, lossy abstraction of the load it is trying to coordinate with.

Telemetry stops being a feature the moment another system depends on its cadence to be safe. — Author

The shift is not philosophical. It is observable in the field. Three things changed between 2022 and 2026: the rise of GW-scale single-tenant campuses, the FERC dynamic-load-model docket1, and the arrival of grid-side autonomous-agent dispatch trials at two ISOs2. Each of those three depends on a telemetry contract the existing procurement model does not produce.

02

Evidence

What does it cost to keep treating telemetry as a feature? Field instrumentation across n = 14 hyperscale and colo sites in 2025 surfaces a consistent gap between what operators believe their headroom is and what sub-cycle waveform capture actually showsm. The shape of that gap is shown in Figure 1.

The pattern is uniform across operator types: hyperscaler, colo, and enterprise on-prem all converge near the same 12–18% mean utilization. That is not a story about any one operator’s discipline. It is a story about what the contract permits the operator to know.

Methodologym

Observation window: 2025-Q1 to 2026-Q1. Utilization-vs-nameplate range (12–18%) is the inter-site mean with 95% CI [11.3%, 18.7%]. Site identities anonymized; aggregated dataset available on request under MNDA.

The instrumentation choice matters. RMS metering at 1-second cadence misses the transient envelope entirely; the gap between “forecast” and “measured peak” in Table 1 cannot be observed at 1 Hz, only inferred. At 8 kHz, the envelope is directly visible, and the inference becomes a measurement3.

04

Implications

If telemetry is a contract rather than a feature, three things change about how the next decade of grid-edge investment should be structured.

One: the contract becomes a precondition for interconnection, not a deliverable of the project. Today, an interconnection request gets approved against a declared nameplate. Tomorrow, the request gets approved against a declared telemetry contract that says “this load will be observable to the operator at this cadence, in this schema, with this latency, available to these recipients.” FERC’s draft dynamic-load-model rule moves in this direction1.

Approve the interconnection against a telemetry contract, not a nameplate. Everything else follows. — Author

Two: the contract becomes legible to autonomous-agent dispatch. If a system at the ISO needs to coordinate sub-second across 200 facilities, it cannot do so against 200 different telemetry definitions. The contract has to be standardized at the schema level (not just the cadence level) so that a dispatch agent can read it without per-site adapters4.

Three: the contract creates a property right the operator can trade. Once the load is legible at the contract level, the operator can sell elasticity into capacity markets at a granularity the markets do not currently support. PJM and ERCOT are both drafting this in 20265.

05

Bottom line

If you are working on AI-grid policy, AI-factory siting, or interconnection-queue reform, three concrete actions that operate on AI-race timescales:

  1. Treat telemetry as the interconnection contract, not as a project deliverable. Sub-cycle cadence (kHz-class) and machine-readable schema are the technical floor.
  2. Standardize the schema before the cadence. A 1-second telemetry feed in a standard schema is more useful to a dispatch agent than an 8 kHz feed in a per-site format.
  3. Make the contract a property right. Operators that meet the contract should be able to monetize their elasticity. Operators that do not meet it should pay the system cost their illegibility creates.
Important. This recommendation assumes the regulator can verify the telemetry contract. Without third-party measurement and verification of the cadence and schema, the contract degrades into self-attestation, which has the same observability properties as the nameplate it replaces.
