Layering the elements

Spectrogram + Lissajous + crosshairs

The spectrogram shows how loads change over time. The Lissajous shows what a load looks like at one moment. Layered together with oscilloscope crosshairs, they create a composite instrument display that reads as "real measurement" to engineers and "beautiful data" to everyone else.

GPU Lissajous in persistence mode (12 traces with phase and amplitude jitter) on a spectrogram bed with oscilloscope crosshairs. The newest trace glows brightest.
Frequency domain

Harmonic fingerprints

64 bars from the 60Hz fundamental through the 32nd harmonic. The decay pattern reveals the load type. Clean power has only the fundamental. A VFD shows strong 5th and 7th harmonics. A fault has broadband energy across all frequencies.

The design model

Quiet Reader +
Precision Instrument

We studied ten contemporary design models. Apple's scroll-driven editorial. Stripe's engineered surfaces. Linear's dark instrument. Craig Mod's quiet reader. Japanese editorial verticality. Swiss rational grids. Dieter Rams' essentialism.

The ones that persist are rooted in functional traditions, not trends. Book design. Instrumentation. Mathematical grids. The ones that date are rooted in aesthetic trends: scroll animations, gradient meshes, dark-mode-everything.

The answer for Verdigris: a hybrid of the Quiet Reader (narrow column, tall sections, images standing alone, typography that whispers) and the Precision Instrument (data visualizations as the visual identity, monospace for measurements, functional color, the "if you know you know" expertise signal).

This hybrid has no expiration date. It is book design plus engineering instrumentation. Both traditions are centuries old.
