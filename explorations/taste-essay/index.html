Can a Design System Describe Taste?

The feedback kept coming back: the website felt off. Not broken. The tokens were correct, the fonts loaded, the spacing was on grid. But something about the feeling was wrong. The pages were technically right and emotionally flat.

This is the story of how we figured out what was missing, and what it took to teach a design system the difference between correct and good.

The starting point

Sixteen colors, two in use

The Verdigris design system had 16 brand color tokens. A full chromatic gradient, carefully interpolated in OKLch: teal to purple to red to yellow and back. It was technically beautiful. It was also almost entirely unused.

The website used brand teal and a neutral gray scale. Every section, every page, every component: teal accent on neutral background. The other 14 colors sat in a JSON file, waiting.

The spacing rules worked. The type scale worked. But they worked in isolation. Nothing in the design system said how color, typography, and spacing should compose together. A human designer would know intuitively that a dark section needs more padding, or that a tinted background changes how body text reads. The system had no way to express that.

16 brand tokens. The bordered swatch is the only one the website used. The rest at 25% opacity: present in the system, absent from every surface.
The first question

What should each color be used for?

The obvious answer was to map colors to emotions. Teal means trust. Purple means sophistication. Red means warmth. We started there.

Three research agents explored what competitors actually do. Stripe uses one purple consistently. Linear is practically monochrome. Datadog uses purple plus orange, but not as "emotional mapping," just as brand identity. None of them assign feelings to hex codes.

But then something shifted. This design system isn't primarily for human designers. It's for machines. Claude builds our pages. The evaluator pipeline judges them. A human designer can look at a detection-focused section and intuit "this should feel more urgent." A machine can't. It needs to be told: detection content uses the energy palette region (purple-to-magenta, hue 313-0, high chroma).

So we mapped colors to content categories, not emotions. The meaning comes from what the content is about, and the color reinforces that meaning.

What went wrong first

Without ceilings, everything becomes a rainbow

The first version of the palette semantics said "use at least 2 palette regions beyond teal." We thought this was encouragement to use the full palette. It was actually an instruction to maximize it.

A machine that reads "minimum 2 regions" interprets it as "use as many as possible." Every section got its own color. Detection was magenta. Results was yellow. Growth was green. Technology was teal. It looked like a children's website.

v1: no ceilings
Final: 70% teal, max 2 regions
Left: what happens when every section uses a different palette region. Right: 70% teal dominance, one earned accent. The brand pillars (Precise, Masterful, Refined) set the ceiling, not arbitrary numbers.

We tightened it three times. First to 60% teal with max 3 regions. Then to 70% teal with max 2, after checking against the brand pillars. "Controlled color palette, no visual clutter" (Masterful). "Subtle use of the brand gradient" (Refined). "Every element earns its place" (Precision). The values stopped being arbitrary when they were justified by the brand itself.

The lesson: every minimum needs a maximum. Machines optimize toward whatever you don't constrain.

The insight that changed direction

Color gives text room to breathe

Thomas said something that reframed the whole approach: "Without more color, text becomes really heavy on screen. Color lets words breathe and gives space and feelings in the narrative."

We'd been treating color as decoration to be rationed. But color is also space. A wall of dark text on white is heavy. A subtle tinted background lightens the cognitive load. A colored label creates a visual landmark that lets the reader scan without reading every word.

We went looking for research to validate or disprove the intuition. What we found changed the rules.

Attention capture
Color is processed in 50-500ms, before conscious thought. A conspicuous target is found 83% faster. The accent section isn't just different. It's where the eye goes first.
Saturation and luxury
Lower saturation enhances perceived luxury. The mechanism: large areas of near-zero chroma (4% tints) against small areas of full chroma (labels, borders). The gap between them is the design.
Teal and readability
Cyan-family backgrounds boost reading accuracy and comfort. Red backgrounds hinder search. Our brand teal isn't just on-brand. It's measurably better for reading.
Color adjacency (Albers)
The same color looks different depending on its neighbors. A magenta label on white reads differently than magenta on neutral.200. Test accent sections in context, not isolation.
Four research findings that changed the composition rules. Pre-attentive capture informed the accent-as-conversion-point rule. Saturation research informed the tint/label contrast. Teal readability confirmed the brand color choice. Albers informed the section flow rules.

Each finding became a rule. Attention capture: the accent section should be the conversion point, not just the section whose content matches a palette region. Saturation contrast: backgrounds stay desaturated, labels stay full chroma. Teal readability: a fortunate alignment between brand identity and reading science. Albers: test in context, not isolation, because neighboring sections change how every color reads.

This changed what the palette semantics were for. Not just "which color for which content" but "how much color does this section earn?" A detection section doesn't just get the energy palette. It gets a specific intensity: a 4% background tint, a colored label, a thin left border. Three quiet signals that together shift the feeling. The section before it gets nothing. The contrast makes the accent land.

What looked wrong

The strobe, and four attempts to fix it

The first specimen alternated dark and light sections: dark hero, light content, dark accent, light content, dark CTA. Five sections, three contrast flips. Thomas looked at it and said "the dark and white switch between sections can be a bit jarring."

He was right. The eye had to re-adapt at every boundary. We tried four different approaches:

A: All-light flow
Accent as a light tint, no dark break. Elegant but potentially invisible.
B: All-dark flow
Premium feel, but heavy for a marketing page.
C: Label color only
Maximum restraint. But a 9px mono label in magenta is hard to see.
D: Layered (final)
Tint + border + label. Three signals that layer. The answer.
Four approaches to section flow, explored and rendered in real time. None of the first three was right alone. D combined the best of A (light flow) and the border from B (structural accent).

The answer was layers. A light tint so subtle you can barely see it. A thin left border in the accent color. A colored label. Each alone is almost invisible. Together they shift the feeling of the section before the reader reads a single word.

This produced a new rule: commit to a lightness direction within the content flow. The hero is dark. The content is light. One dark accent moment is allowed (the hero and footer don't count). Within the light flow, vary with subtle tonal shifts (white to neutral.200), not lightness inversions.

Competitive assessment

What the best B2B brands actually do

We studied six companies: Stripe, Linear, Datadog, Grafana, Vercel, and Cloudflare. The question was whether our approach to palette semantics and composition was novel, standard, or misguided.

The finding was consistent: the best B2B brands succeed through restraint, not chromatic range. And none of them do what we assumed they do.

Stripe
Famous WebGL mesh gradient, but it's purely decorative atmosphere. Individual sections don't get themed colors. Product pages use dark navy + white + one accent. Never assigns emotions to colors.
Linear
Practically monochrome. Desaturated blue as primary, occasional purple in gradients. Their design system is explicitly monochrome-first. Minimal color, high contrast, content-forward. Restraint as sophistication.
Datadog
Purple brand with orange CTA accent. Not "emotional mapping," just brand identity. Their DRUIDS design system uses functional semantics (primary, success, danger), not emotional ones.
Vercel
Almost entirely black and white. Blue used sparingly for links and CTAs. Demonstrates that B2B credibility can come from absence of color.
Cloudflare
Orange brand with neutral grays. Published a detailed blog on building their color system focused entirely on accessibility and developer predictability. Zero mention of emotional semantics.
What we learned
Nobody maps colors to emotions. The best brands use functional semantics + restraint. Our approach aligns: content categories (not emotions), 70% teal dominance, max 2 accent regions. Restraint is the common denominator.
Six B2B competitors studied. The pattern: restraint, functional color, and generous whitespace. None of them use "emotional color mapping." Neither do we.
The composition matrix

One framework, thirteen cells

A homepage composes differently from a dashboard. An ad banner composes differently from an email. And the same surface can have different purposes: a web page selling the product (persuade) vs a web page explaining the API (inform) vs a web page showing analytics (analyze).

We defined seven purpose types. Each has its own composition logic: different color budgets, spacing scales, type hierarchies, and rhythms.

Persuade
Sell, convince, inspire
Narrative arc, generous whitespace, earned accent. Homepage, landing pages.
Inform
Explain, document, teach
Steady rhythm, tight spacing, color as wayfinding only. Docs, blog, case studies.
Analyze
Show data, monitor, compare
Full palette, compact grid, functional color. Dashboards, data viz.
Convert
Drive a single action
Tight funnel, single CTA, max 3 tiers. Pricing, signup, demo request.
Identify
Brand recognition in seconds
Teal only, Z-pattern, single moment. Ads, hardware, trade show.
Narrate
Tell a story with depth
Episode + figure pairs, magazine pacing, color in figures not backgrounds. Essays, retrospectives. This page is an example.
Assist
AI interaction
Flat layout, competence over personality, provenance markers. Chat, embedded AI content.
Instruct
Machine-to-machine
No visual output. The design is the structure of the YAML rules themselves.
Eight purpose types, each with different composition rules. The same surface (a web page) can serve different purposes. This essay uses narrate. The homepage uses persuade. Docs use inform. The rules are different for each.

Six research agents explored award-winning design across these surfaces simultaneously. Some of what they found: B2B display ads that win at Cannes treat the banner as a brand impression, not a conversion tool. The best enterprise emails look like well-formatted text, not marketing collateral. Dashboards use the full 16-color palette because data visualization is what the gradient was designed for. Pricing pages need exactly 3 tiers (more than 6 drops conversion from 30% to 3%).

We also discovered a surface that doesn't exist in any design system we could find: LLM chat interfaces. No established best practices. What this doesn't do

The framework is not a panacea

We ran three adversarial review passes. They caught 12 issues, including a rule that said 30% when the actual constraint was 70%, a specimen that contradicted its own YAML, broken dark mode rendering, and AI writing artifacts that survived two manual cleanup passes.

The reviews were valuable. But they also showed the limits of the process. Here's what the framework doesn't do:

It doesn't replace iteration. The strobe problem is the clearest example. The first specimen alternated dark and light sections. No rule flagged it. Thomas looked at it and said "the dark and white switch between sections can be a bit jarring." He was right. We wrote the section-flow rules after the fact, to codify what his eye caught instantly. The framework carries decisions forward, but someone has to make the first call.

It doesn't eliminate bad output. This page, built to demonstrate the framework, had 12 violations of its own composition rules when the adversarial reviewer checked. H2 sections with 4rem padding instead of 5rem. A closing section with marketing-style pill buttons. Hardcoded white backgrounds that broke in dark mode. Knowing the rules and following them are different things.

It doesn't create taste. It encodes taste that already exists. The design work that established the visual identity came first. The brand pillars grew from those choices. The composition rules formalized the pillars into constraints. But someone had to make the original judgment calls, and someone still has to say "that doesn't feel right" when the rules pass but the output is off.

The adversarial review process itself has limits. It catches contradictions (a rule says 70%, the docs say 30%). It catches structural issues (broken dark mode, missing schema fields). It doesn't catch whether a page feels like Verdigris. It doesn't have taste. It has rules about taste.

What the framework does: it sets boundaries so the worst choices get caught early. It compresses iteration cycles. And it leaves space within the boundaries for the creative judgment that no rule can encode. The space between "follows all the rules" and "feels right" is where design happens.

What was uniquely AI-enabled: 6 research agents explored award-winning design across ads, email, dashboards, slides, docs, and LLM interfaces simultaneously. Adversarial review agents caught rules contradicting their own specimens. The design system is itself a machine-consumable artifact: AI agents read the YAML to build pages, the evaluator validates the output.

This isn't about replacing designers. The design work that established the visual identity is what the brand pillars encode. It's about encoding taste so it can be applied consistently and iterated on faster than any team could manage alone.

The hardware and industrial design cell exists in the matrix but is empty. Brand alignment for physical products without a painful iteration process is an open opportunity.

Feedback

Three things the essay didn't answer

We shared the essay internally and got the kind of feedback that changes the shape of the project. Three questions surfaced that the framework had no answer for.

Where are the brand values?

Precision, Masterful, Refined, Pioneering. They show up in the taste boundaries to justify the 70% teal ceiling. But they don't do structural work. Should each composition cell trace to a brand value?

We decided: no. Brand values are expressed through concrete guardrails, not declared as labels. The strobe problem was solved by max_lightness_jump: 0.15, not by writing "Refined means no strobing." The coupling rules ARE the brand values in mechanical form. If you add primary_brand_value: "Refined" to a composition cell, an AI agent now has a justification layer above the concrete rules. "I'm making this more Refined" becomes a reason to override a padding minimum. The concrete rules are the immune system. Brand-value labels are an attack surface.

How do pages earn trust?

Infrastructure buyers operate in tight culture. High consequence of failure, strong norms around vendor selection. They need conformity signals first, craft second, vision third. Leading with Pioneering is a loose-culture pitch that works at a startup competition and terrifies an infrastructure buyer.

With 60%+ of traffic arriving from LLMs with intent already shaped, each page has to do all the emotional work by itself. So we added surface registers: behavioral descriptions of how each purpose type earns trust within its own scroll. Persuade pages open with restraint and earn permission for boldness through the narrative arc. Inform pages earn trust through precision. Narrate pages earn trust through honesty. Not brand-value labels. Trust-earning sequences.

What makes this visually unmistakable?

The essay solved "how do we use our 16 colors." It did not solve "what visual element is uniquely ours." We capture electrical behavior at 8,000 samples per second. That data, made visible, is a design asset no competitor can replicate. The composition rules encode color restraint. A signature motif is what makes it ours.

We don't know the answer yet. Real waveform data might be beautiful at hero scale or it might be noise. Stylizing it risks looking like every other tech company's decorative wave. We opened a research spike to find out. If real data can be beautiful, it becomes the visual signature. If it can't, it stays a proof point. The decision depends on seeing it, not theorizing about it.

What's next

Try it. Tell us what's off.

If you work on the product, pick a component and check it against the dashboard rules. If you work on marketing, run the next email or ad through its composition cell. If you work on analytics, compare a dashboard view to the specimen. If you lead the company, look at the before/after at the top. Does the "after" feel like the brand you want?

The framework sets boundaries so poor choices get caught. But the best choices still need people who care about the feeling. That's what makes this a design system and not just a style guide.

The obvious next step: apply this to verdigris.co. The persuade specimen now demonstrates a full page composition from hook to close. That's the reference implementation. See what the composition rules produce against what exists today.

This essay exposed two gaps. The first: no composition type fit an editorial essay, so we created narrate. The second: the framework encoded how to use color and space, but not what makes Verdigris visually unmistakable. The waveform question is open. The framework grew again because it encountered surfaces it couldn't serve. That might be the best evidence that the approach works.
